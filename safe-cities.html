Step 01 · The Gatekeeping Problem

Approaching the police is not free

For women facing gender-based violence in Pakistan, the path to reporting requires physical presence at a police station. This creates multiple compounding barriers:

  • Leaving home is visible to household members who may be abusers or enforcers of silence
  • Police stations are predominantly male spaces — socially stigmatizing to visit for GBV matters
  • Travel costs and logistics in districts with poor mobility are substantial
  • First-contact officers exercise significant discretionary power to admit or dismiss a complaint
Step 02 · The Reform

Women's Virtual Police Stations: a digital entry point

Launched in February 2024 by the Punjab Safe Cities Authority, the WVPS fundamentally changes how women can access the justice system:

  • Remote reporting via the centralized 15 emergency helpline — no physical visit required
  • Complaints logged directly into a digital case management system (CMS), bypassing local discretion
  • Privacy and distance from household surveillance at point of first contact
  • Case tracked digitally through the downstream pipeline into FIR registration
Step 03 · The Research Question

Does lowering the entry barrier change justice outcomes?

The WVPS is a large-scale natural experiment. We ask: when digital technology removes the physical gatekeeping barrier, what happens throughout the justice pipeline?

  • Does overall GBV reporting increase — or just shift channels?
  • Does remote reporting substitute for walk-in reporting, or is the gain additive?
  • Do more reports translate into more formal FIR registrations downstream?
  • Are effects stronger in more conservative districts where stigma barriers are higher?

Abstract

This paper studies a large-scale digital reform in Pakistan that changes the entry point to justice: Women's Virtual Police Stations (WVPS). The WVPS allows women to report Gender-Based Violence (GBV) remotely through a centralized phone-based intake system and digital case management. This reduces the need for in-person visits and potentially limits local discretion at first contact.

We combine high-frequency administrative data on police calls and complaints with downstream records on case registration and escalation to examine how digital access reshapes reporting and early-stage justice delivery.

The Core Problem: In many low-capacity states, the criminal justice system fails long before a case reaches a courtroom. For women facing GBV, approaching the police requires traveling to a station under household monitoring, facing social stigma, and confronting discretionary gatekeeping.
Research Design

Data & Methodology

We leverage administrative data spanning 2021–2024, combined with a Difference-in-Differences event-study design around the February 2024 WVPS launch.

PUCAR 15 — Emergency Calls

The centralized emergency response system, capturing real-time call-initiated reports. This is the primary data source for remote GBV reporting through the 15 helpline.

Complaint Management System (CMS)

The digital tracking platform that logs walk-in complaints and follows case progression into First Information Reports (FIRs) and further escalation.

High-Frequency Panel

Data spans 2021 through 2024, providing a comprehensive panel of police station-period observations — enabling pre- and post-WVPS comparisons.

Event-Study DiD Design

We exploit heterogeneous exposure across police stations. High-exposure stations (top 30% of pre-program reporting volume by emergency calls) serve as the primary treatment group, compared against lower-exposure stations before and after the February 2024 rollout.

For full methodology, identification assumptions, and robustness checks, see the paper: doi.org/10.7274/31919538
Evidence

Event-Study Results

Difference-in-differences event study plots showing effects relative to the WVPS launch (t = 0). Pre-trends test the parallel trends assumption. Charts are illustrative reconstructions of paper figures.

01 · Remote vs Walk-in
Two Channels, Opposite Directions
When the WVPS launched, remote phone-based reporting surged while walk-in visits to police stations declined. This is the substitution effect — women switching to safer reporting pathways, not just reporting more.
02 · Net Effect
A Net Increase in Reporting
Even accounting for substitution, total reported GBV rises after the reform. The pre-trend is flat, confirming the post-treatment increase is caused by the WVPS — not pre-existing trends.
03 · FIR Pipeline
The Pipeline Bottleneck
Formal case registration (FIRs) also increases, but the effect is smaller than the reporting increase. Digital intake alone cannot fix the full system — discretion at later stages still constrains justice delivery.
04 · Heterogeneity
Where Effects Are Concentrated
Effects are largest for Domestic Violence and Harassment — the categories where household control and social stigma are most binding. This is consistent with the WVPS reducing barriers specific to those crimes.

Summary

Key Findings

Four main results from the difference-in-differences analysis of the WVPS rollout.

Increased GBV Reporting

The WVPS increases reported GBV incidents. Effects are heavily concentrated in categories where social stigma and household control are plausibly the most binding constraints for women attempting to reach police.

Substitution of Reporting Pathways

The reform fundamentally changes the composition and pathway of reporting. Remote reporting via the 15 helpline rises dramatically while walk-in reporting falls — a substantial substitution in how women engage the police, not a purely mechanical increase.

Downstream Pipeline Movement

Digital intake produces measurable downstream movement: formal FIR registration and follow-up actions increase. This occurs with delay, consistent with existing capacity constraints and case-processing frictions at the local level.

Heterogeneity by Social Conservatism

In highly conservative districts, remote reporting surged but formal FIRs did not increase proportionately — the WVPS overcame the initial contact barrier but local norms still prevented legal escalation. In less conservative districts, the remote reporting spike translated more fully into formal FIRs.
